I — Why this exists
The founding premise
"Life is the intersection between extremely hard things and extremely beautiful things. Tri Vananda is built in that space."
The context we cannot ignore
Thailand is aging faster than it is growing wealthy. Families are fracturing under economic pressure. A generation of children is growing up distracted and anxious. Our coastlines and coral reefs are losing the battle. These are not abstract problems — they are the conditions our residents carry with them when they arrive.
The response we chose
Not to build another resort. Not to sell another concept. But to design a living environment — grounded in science, rooted in Thai culture, built for multiple generations — where the difficulty of life and the beauty of life can be held together. Permanently. Meaningfully. Across time.

II — The hard things
II — The hard things

01
Growing Old Before Growing Rich
Thailand is aging faster than almost any economy in Southeast Asia — without the wealth buffer of Singapore, Japan, or Korea. By 2023, 20% of the population was over 60. Birth rates have declined at a CAGR of −1.94% over two decades. GDP growth has averaged 5.34% annually. Only 5% of elderly Thais have sufficient income to save for retirement. The younger generation is being asked to carry more than any generation before them.
20% over 60 · Birth rate among lowest in ASEAN · Only 5% confident in retirement
02
The Anxious Generation
Thai children average 4 hours of daily screen time — double the WHO recommendation, and more than any comparable ASEAN peer group. Social media exposure is linked directly to rising teenage anxiety. A generation growing up with devices instead of territory, with algorithms instead of community, with performance pressure instead of play.
4 hrs/day screen time · Double WHO limit · Anxiety rising across all age groups
03
A Climate Crisis with a 30-Year Bill
Coral bleaching has damaged over 85 sq km of reef in the Gulf of Thailand. Rainfall patterns swung from a 40-year high to a near-record drought within three years. Tourism — one fifth of Thailand's GDP — is under direct threat. So are the 12 million farmers who make up one sixth of the population. The land we build on is already absorbing consequences we did not create.
85 sq km reef damage · Tourism = 1/5 of GDP · 12M farmers at risk

01
Community-Based Solutions to Health
Harvard's 85-year happiness study found that strong social relationships are a greater predictor of long-term health than genetics, social class, or education. People with deep social bonds retain memory function longer and live measurably healthier lives. Blue Zones research confirms it: ikigai — a sense of purpose within a community — is the most consistent factor in populations that live past 100.
Harvard Happiness Study · Blue Zones / Ikigai · Social connection as medicine
02
Conscious Parenting
Dr. Shefali Tsabary's work demonstrates that children flourish not when parents control outcomes, but when parents are present, emotionally aware, and willing to listen. Thai culture and Buddhist practice already hold this understanding — in the values of harmony, respect, and intergenerational care that have shaped family life here for centuries.
Dr. Shefali Tsabary · The Conscious Parent · Rooted in Thai Buddhist tradition
03
Regenerative Investment
Global investment in clean energy exceeded USD 2 trillion in 2024 — surpassing fossil fuels for the first time. The ASEAN green bond market reached USD 10 billion by late 2022. Thailand ranked 24th on the Climate Change Performance Index 2025 and is on course for carbon neutrality by 2050. Capital and purpose are moving in the same direction.
Clean energy $2T+ · ASEAN green bonds $10B · Thailand CCPI ranked 24th

V — The origin
Where the name came from
Built from
personal
understanding.

This project began with illness — a family's encounter with cancer, and a recovery that came not from medicine alone, but from a change in how they lived. That experience is not a marketing story. It is the reason Tri Vananda exists.

We did not begin with a plot of land in Phuket. We began with a question: what kind of environment actually helps people live longer, and better, and more connected to the people they love? Everything that followed was an attempt to answer it honestly.

01
Community-Based Health
Proud Family hospitality, Be Wellness programming, and Local Roots Market — a living social infrastructure designed to produce the longevity outcomes that Harvard and the Blue Zones identify as determinants of a long, well life.
Wellness economy: $6.3T globally · Larger than Thailand's entire GDP
02
Conscious Parenting Spaces
Manorah Grand Hall, rooted in Southern Thai drama and culture. The Tweenie Club for children to explore and connect. Pru Jampa Farm for families to work the land together. Environments that make mindfulness a daily practice, not a retreat activity.
Clinique La Prairie partnership · Global health resort at Tri Vananda
03
Multigenerational Design
Spaces that work for an eight-year-old and a seventy-year-old in the same daily life. 85% of land returned to nature. Community built to hold families across ordinary time — not just extraordinary occasions.
60% green · 25% water · 10% building · 5% infrastructure
04
Regenerative Investment
Carbon-neutral villas on a 30-year path to net-zero: passive design reduces 40%, solar roofs a further 15%, green land absorption eliminates the rest. Every rai absorbs 2.5 tonnes of CO₂ per year. Ownership that contributes to the planet.
100% → 60% → 45% → Net-Zero · 1 Rai = 2.5t CO₂e / year

Tri Vananda creates a wellness-focused lifestyle designed for longevity. Anchored by a Health Resort by Clinique La Prairie — one of the world's most respected preventive health institutions — and layered with meaningful intergenerational experiences, families can embrace mindful living and the kind of well-being that accumulates, rather than depletes, over time.

This is not an environmental offset — it is an architectural commitment made permanent in the land itself, for the children who will inherit it.
Net-negative carbon · 30 years · One generation

VIII — The standards we hold
What we will not compromise on
These are not
aspirations. They are
operating conditions.
Nature as Infrastructure
Every decision — architectural, operational, horticultural — is evaluated against what the land requires. 85% of the site is returned to nature. The green area is sized precisely to absorb the carbon emissions of every residence over 30 years. Nature is not a feature we add. It is the frame everything else sits inside.
Community as Medicine
Social architecture is designed intentionally — not to fill the calendar, but to create the conditions for genuine encounter. The science of Blue Zones and Harvard's longitudinal research confirms what good cultures have always known: belonging is not a luxury. It is a biological requirement. The unscripted afternoon matters as much as the curated programme.
Time as the Unit of Value
We are not building a place to visit. We are building a place to return to. The measure of success is not occupancy or revenue — it is whether families choose to come back out of preference, not obligation. Whether children grow up with roots. Whether a grandparent's final years are spent well. These are the returns that matter.
